Descriptions and Sources

The objective of this project was to delve further into existing research around the relationship between crime and temperature. We know from prior studies that the temperature of a given day does not linearly correlate to the expectation of crime, however, we hypothesize that crime area (based on Chicagoâ€™s Community Area), historical crime by crime type, population and potentially an indicator (like the Hardship Index) could provide further insight to the relationship between crime and temperature.

We sourced our data through the Chicago Data Portal (https://data.cityofchicago.org/Public-Safety/Crimes-2001-to-present/ijzp-q8t2), the Bureau of Labor Statistics (https://www.bls.gov/data/), OpenWeather App (https://openweathermap.org)and observed the various sets of data. We then took this data, reframed it based on machine learning models that we researched. We took a look at linear, naive Bayes and ultimately took advantage of building a neural network with TensorFlow. We found that linear regression (depending on the variables) and TensorFlow produced conclusive results. As we went through the data we realized the importance of other factors like humidity, precipitation, community area, number of arrests, domestic versus non-domestic crime factors and population etc... mattered to bringing further precision. Ultimately due to time, we were unable to fully incorporate all these factors with a model that provided a predictive value for crime. This is something that we would aspire to complete in future developments.

For this model we have selected the top five crimes (as determined based on occurrences from 2001- 2019) in the Chicago area. This resulted in roughly +3 million rows of data. The model has averaged the temperatures for the date of crime, and averaged the number of crime type per day. We then took the approach of using TensorFlow and linear model data to run the predictive engine in the model. Go to the drop-down and select your crime type and temperature.
